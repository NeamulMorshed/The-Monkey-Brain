## Factual summary

Bush proposed the **memex**: a hypothetical desk-sized electromechanical device for storing
and retrieving a person's books, records, and correspondence on microfilm. Its defining
feature was **associative trails** — the user could link any two items together and follow
that link later, mimicking how the mind associates ideas, rather than retrieving information
through a fixed hierarchical filing scheme. Bush framed it as "an enlarged intimate
supplement to" personal memory: privately curated, built up by the owner over a working
lifetime. He described the mechanism (photocells, coded indexing, a stylus for annotation)
but not who would maintain such a store at scale — retrieval was solved, upkeep was not.

The essay is widely credited as a conceptual precursor to hypertext and the World Wide Web;
Douglas Engelbart, Ted Nelson, and Tim Berners-Lee all cited it as an influence.
